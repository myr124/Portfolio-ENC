Multi-Agent AI Systems for Emergency Response

The finalized proposal is included below in a web-friendly format.

Project Title and Faculty Mentor

Intelligence in Crisis: Multi-Agent AI Systems for Emergency Response Decision-Making Using Large Language Models and Parallel Agents

Faculty Mentor: Dr. Mubarak Shah, Director of the Institute for AI, Trustee Chair Professor, Department of Computer Science, College of Engineering & Computer Science, University of Central Florida.

Project Objective or Aim

This project explores how multi-agent artificial intelligence systems can simulate crisis scenarios and improve emergency response decision-making. The study investigates how multiple AI agents, each assigned a role such as civilian, responder, or government official, interact within a dynamic disaster environment.

The central research question is: how can multi-agent AI simulations enhance the speed, coordination, and effectiveness of decision-making during high-pressure crisis situations? By developing and evaluating a prototype powered by large language models and parallel agent execution, the project aims to support training, planning, and real-time strategy development for emergency management.

Project Background and Significance

Emergency response scenarios, including natural disasters, public health crises, and large-scale accidents, require rapid and coordinated decisions under uncertainty. Delays or poor coordination can lead to loss of life, inefficient resource allocation, and long-term consequences.

Traditional training approaches, such as tabletop exercises and static simulations, often fail to capture the complexity and unpredictability of real crises. Recent advances in large language models and multi-agent systems offer a more adaptive way to model dynamic environments and realistic communication.

Multi-agent systems are useful because independent agents can interact in a shared environment and produce coordinated or emergent behavior. When combined with language models, these agents can simulate more human-like communication and decision-making. This project applies those ideas to emergency response training and decision support.

The significance of the research is its potential to improve preparedness and real-time response. More interactive simulations can help identify coordination patterns, decision-making strategies, and system-level behavior during crises. The work also supports interdisciplinary collaboration across public safety, engineering, and computer science.

Research Methods

The project will be completed through three main phases: system development, simulation testing, and performance evaluation.

  1. System development: A prototype simulation platform will model a dynamic crisis environment using autonomous AI agents. Each agent will represent a role, communicate through natural language, and participate in coordinated decision-making. The system will use a lightweight full-stack architecture with backend coordination services and a frontend interface for visualizing agent behavior.
  2. Simulation testing: The system will be tested with crisis scenarios that vary in complexity and scale. Response time, communication efficiency, and coordination effectiveness will be recorded across multiple simulation runs.
  3. Performance evaluation: Results will be analyzed to evaluate the system's effectiveness and identify patterns in decision-making and coordination. The system may then be refined to improve agent interaction and performance.

The semester timeline includes weeks 1-3 for research and system design, weeks 4-8 for development, weeks 9-12 for testing and data collection, and weeks 13-15 for analysis and final reporting.

Expected Outcome

The primary outcome will be a functional multi-agent AI simulation platform for emergency response scenarios. Users will be able to observe how responders, civilians, and coordinating agencies interact, communicate, and respond to changing conditions.

The project will also produce a report documenting the system design, development process, and evaluation results. The report will focus on coordination efficiency, communication patterns, and decision-making effectiveness during simulations.

The results may be shared through a university research event at UCF, either as a research poster or a live demonstration. Long term, the project could support better training and planning tools for crisis response.

Preliminary Work and Experience

I have experience developing AI-driven systems and full-stack applications that align with this project. Most notably, I contributed to Emergent, a multi-agent crisis simulation platform developed for KnightHacks. I focused on backend development and agent coordination, helping design a system where multiple AI agents interact in a dynamic environment.

Emergent won Best Overall Hack at KnightHacks and placed second in the Google ADK Agentic Challenge. It was also recognized in the UCF Innovation Challenge, which helped validate its potential as a product and its relevance to real-world stakeholders.

I have also worked as a Software Engineer Intern at Siemens Energy, where I developed internal tools to support engineering workflows and system analysis. That experience strengthened my ability to design scalable systems and work with technical teams.
